# Rates & Earnings

Rate questions deserve numbers, not vibes — but honest numbers come in ranges, because the three research reports behind this site (and the platforms they surveyed) genuinely disagree at the edges. Below: the synthesized picture, *why* the ranges are wide, and a practical method for setting your own number.

## Freelance hourly rates (USD, 2024–2026 snapshot)

| Specialization | Entry / low | Typical mid | High end | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Manual / functional | $5–12 | $12–25 | $35–60 | The $5 end is the commodity trap; the $35+ end is manual testers with niche domain skill |
| Usability / UAT | $5–15 | $10–20 | $30 (or per-study $15–30) | Volume game on study platforms |
| Game testing | $5–10 | $10–20 | $30 | Lowest band; oversupplied with enthusiasts |
| Mobile app testing | $10–18 | $18–35 | $45+ | Real-device coverage commands the premium |
| API testing | $15–25 | $25–45 | $55+ | Best skill-to-rate ratio for non-programmers |
| Web automation | $15–30 | $30–60 | $90–150+ | The widest band — framework + CI skill decides where you land |
| Performance / load | $25–40 | $40–80 | $120+ | Scarce supply |
| Security testing | $35–50 | $50–120 | $200+ | Different career, really |

## Full-time remote salaries (USD/year, US-market roles)

| Level | Range across sources | Midpoint feel |
|---|---|---|
| Junior QA (0–2 yrs) | $42–100K | ~$60–80K |
| Mid-level (2–4 yrs) | $50–140K | ~$85–105K |
| Senior (5+ yrs) | $70–185K | ~$110–135K |
| Lead / Manager | $95–250K | ~$130–175K |

Automation-focused roles consistently price 30–50% above manual-only at the same level; US-company remote roles pay multiples of locally-anchored salaries elsewhere — which is the entire reason this site exists.

## Geographic arbitrage — the honest mechanics

Clients on open marketplaces routinely scale offers by freelancer location. The Gemini reference's snapshot of typical client hourly offers:

| Freelancer region | Typical offered rate |
|---|---|
| North America | ~$95/hr |
| Western Europe | ~$85/hr |
| Eastern Europe | ~$45/hr |
| Latin America | ~$35/hr |
| South / Southeast Asia | ~$25/hr |

Read this correctly: it's unfair, *and* it's the arbitrage that makes the opportunity. Testing skills are perfectly portable — a Playwright suite from Dhaka is identical to one from Denver. A skilled automation engineer in a low-cost region charging "senior South Asia" rates ($30–45/hr) is simultaneously a bargain for a US client and a top-decile local income. The gap also narrows as you climb: location pricing dominates the commodity tier, while at the specialized end clients increasingly pay for the *person*, not the postcode. One more reason to [exit the commodity tier fast](./01-market-overview.md#the-bifurcation--and-your-strategy).

## Why the published ranges disagree so much

Worth understanding, because it inoculates you against both despair ("$5/hr?!") and delusion ("$90/hr soon!"): averages mix regions with 10x cost differences; "QA tester" spans button-clicker to SDET; platform-listed rates differ from *won* rates; survey data skews toward those who answer surveys; and hourly vs. fixed-price vs. salaried numbers aren't comparable. Whenever you see a single confident number for "QA freelance rates," someone has averaged apples, oranges, and furniture.

## Setting *your* rate — a method, not a number

1. **Find your reference band.** Filter your primary platform for your specialization + region; record the rates of 10 profiles with strong reviews doing work you can credibly do *today*. That's the market's answer.
2. **Position inside the band by proof, not hope.** No reviews yet → bottom third of the band (NOT below it — $3/hr signals desperation and attracts the worst clients; the references' guidance converges on **$5–10/hr manual / $10–18/hr with skills** as a credible newcomer floor depending on region). Portfolio + crowdtesting record → middle. Reviews + niche → top.
3. **Plan the ladder in advance.** A working default: after reviews #3, #10, and each retainer win, raise 20–30% for *new* clients. Existing clients get smaller, notice-period raises — scripts in [Pricing & Contracts](../05-freelancing/04-pricing-and-contracts.md).
4. **Sanity-check against your floor.** Compute your minimum viable hourly (living costs + taxes + fees + unbillable time ÷ billable hours). Platform fees (~10% Upwork, 20% Fiverr), payment fees (~2%), and the ~30–50% of time spent bidding/learning/administrating mean a "$15/hr" gig nets far less. Don't price below the floor for longer than a deliberate review-buying phase.

:::tip The uncomfortable truth about your first three contracts

Your first contracts are not income — they're **paid marketing**. You're buying reviews, a Job Success Score, and proof-of-work, probably at an effective rate that would be unacceptable later. That's fine *if it's a deliberate 60–90 day phase with an exit ladder*, and corrosive if it quietly becomes your identity. Decide the end date of your "cheap era" before it starts.

:::

## Earnings trajectory (composite of the three references' roadmaps)

Realistic monthly income for a disciplined beginner: months 0–2: **$0** · months 2–4: **$50–300** (crowdtesting) · months 4–6: **$200–500** (first contracts) · months 6–12: **$500–2,000** (reviews + early specialization) · year 2 with automation/API specialization: **$1,500–4,000+**. Faster happens; slower is common; the variable that moves the curve most is how early you escape commodity manual work.
