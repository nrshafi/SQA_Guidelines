# Platforms & Marketplaces

There are four distinct *kinds* of venue for remote QA work, and they reward different things. Beginners waste months treating them as interchangeable. They're not — they form a **ladder**, and the smart move is climbing it in order.

## The four tiers at a glance

| Tier | Examples | You're paid for | Entry barrier | Best at stage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Crowdtesting** | uTest, Test IO, Testbirds, Testlio | Approved bugs / test cases / hours | **Low** — open academies | Months 1–4: first real experience |
| **Open marketplaces** | Upwork, Fiverr, Freelancer.com, PeoplePerHour, Guru | Winning client contracts | Moderate-high (competition) | Months 4+: first clients |
| **Curated job boards** | LinkedIn, We Work Remotely, Remote.co, FlexJobs, RemoteOK, Wellfound | Landing salaried/contract roles | High (experience expected) | Year 1+: stable income |
| **Elite networks** | Toptal, Lemon.io, Arc | Pre-vetted premium contracts | **Very high** (multi-stage screening; Toptal markets a ~3% acceptance rate) | Year 2+: premium rates |

## Tier 1 — Crowdtesting: the apprenticeship nobody tells beginners about

Crowdtesting platforms distribute real companies' test cycles to a global tester crowd. Pay is modest, but they solve the beginner's fatal problem — **no experience, no portfolio, no references** — better than anything else, and they're the strongest original recommendation this site makes for months 1–4.

| Platform | Model | Beginner path | Reality check |
|---|---|---|---|
| **uTest** (Applause) | Pay per approved bug / test case | uTest Academy: free sandbox cycles that *teach* professional bug reporting | The biggest crowd; invitations depend on your device profile + rating. Fill the profile exhaustively — every device you own is an invitation magnet |
| **Test IO** | Pay per approved bug, severity-scaled | Test IO Academy onboarding | Invalid/duplicate bugs lower your rating and future invites — the [bug-report craft](../02-core-craft/02-bug-reports.md) literally is your wage here |
| **Testbirds** | Fixed-fee studies + per-bug | Open registration, EU-centric | Strong in usability and **accessibility** (EAA-driven) studies — a niche with [rising demand](./01-market-overview.md) |
| **Testlio** | **Hourly-paid** cycles | Vetting assessment (acceptance ~3%) | The crowdtesting platform that pays like a job; worth re-applying to after 6 months of uTest ratings |

What crowdtesting actually pays you: first money ($50–300/month is realistic by months 2–4), but more importantly **ratings, device-lab discipline, professional bug-report reps, and résumé lines** ("found and reported 60+ approved defects across 14 commercial apps via uTest"). Treat it as a paid apprenticeship, not a destination — the ceiling is low and per-bug payment punishes thorough-but-unlucky testing.

## Tier 2 — Open marketplaces: where first clients live

**Upwork** is the deepest QA market (hundreds of open QA listings at any moment — the references counted ~875 "QA tester" jobs at one snapshot). Hourly or fixed-price; bidding costs Connects (a small real-money stake per proposal, which is *good* — it thins lazy competition). Success is a numbers-times-quality game: tight niche profile + [personalized proposals](../05-freelancing/03-winning-proposals.md) + entry-level filters at first. Expect fee ~10% (check current schedule; Upwork has used 5/10/20% tiers, flat 10%, and 0–15% variable models over the years).

**Fiverr** inverts the model: you publish productized "gigs" and buyers come to you. Works for QA when the gig is *concrete and scoped*: "I will functionally test your Shopify checkout on 6 real devices and deliver a prioritized bug report — from $50." Fiverr keeps 20%. Discovery favors specific niches over "I will QA your app." Slow to rank; pairs well with active Upwork bidding rather than replacing it.

**Freelancer.com / PeoplePerHour / Guru** — smaller QA volume, aggressive price pressure (Freelancer's "contests" pay only the winner — generally skip those). Worth a profile if a niche fits (PeoplePerHour skews UK/EU), not worth your prime attention.

## Tier 3 — Curated job boards: salaried remote QA

**LinkedIn** carries the most remote QA roles of any single venue, plus the recruiter inbound that compounds over time — optimizing your profile there is covered in [Getting Hired](../06-remote-work/02-getting-hired.md). **We Work Remotely / Remote.co / FlexJobs / RemoteOK / Wellfound (startups)** list genuine remote positions, mostly expecting 2+ years of demonstrable experience — which crowdtesting plus a few marketplace contracts can legitimately constitute. No platform fees; traditional interviews instead.

## Tier 4 — Elite networks

**Toptal, Lemon.io, Arc** pre-vet talent and sell trust to clients at premium blended rates (often $60+/hr billed). Multi-stage screens: English, live technical interviews, test projects. Apply when you can pass a senior bar cold — a failed screen typically locks you out for months. One strong year of real contracts first is the sensible sequencing.

## Choosing your two

Spreading across six platforms produces zero traction on all of them. The strategy that works:

1. **Months 1–4:** uTest + Test IO (experience, ratings, first dollars) while building the [portfolio](../05-freelancing/01-portfolio.md).
2. **Months 4–9:** Upwork as primary (where first real clients are won) + keep the best crowdtesting stream alive. Optionally park one well-made Fiverr gig as passive inbound.
3. **Months 9+:** add LinkedIn/WWR applications (stability) or double down on marketplace specialization; consider elite networks at the 18–24 month mark.

:::tip Platform risk — the part most guides omit

Any platform can suspend your account, change its fees, or algorithm-bury you overnight, and your reviews are not portable. From your first client onward, practice **platform-independence hygiene**: deliver excellence, build a portfolio that exists *off*-platform (GitHub/Notion — see [Portfolio](../05-freelancing/01-portfolio.md)), and let satisfied clients know (within each platform's rules — solicit carefully, Upwork restricts off-platform contact with *platform-met* clients) that you also exist on LinkedIn. Long-term, your goal is for platforms to be lead sources, not landlords. More in [Scaling Up](../07-career/03-scaling-up.md).

:::
